Chapter 1

The Two Engineering Organizations Most of Us Actually Work In

(And Why This Book Starts Here)

Before we talk about AI. Before we talk about future-ready organizations. Before we talk about what needs to change.

I want to ask you to pause for a moment and recognize the world you already live in.

This book is not written from theory. It is written from experience — from working inside engineering organizations for more than two decades, organizations that shipped real software, ran real businesses, missed deadlines, made trade-offs, and survived despite their imperfections.

I have worked in traditional enterprises, reasonably mature product organizations, startups, large-scale platforms, and turnaround situations. I have seen projects fail. I have seen teams fail. And I have seen teams succeed not because they were ideal, but because they adapted enough to keep moving.

This book starts here because no meaningful transformation starts without honesty about the present.

Why talk about engineering organizations at all?

Most engineering books start with how things should work. This book deliberately does not. Instead, it starts with how engineering organizations actually work — because that is where most people are standing today.

In practice, most companies fall into one of two broad patterns:

  • The Traditional Engineering Organization
  • The Reasonably Mature — but Imperfect — Agile Organization

These are not judgments. They are descriptions of stable realities.

The shared assumption

Although these two organizations look different on the surface, they share a deeper assumption:

Software delivery is bottlenecked by humans writing code.

This assumption explains specialization, handoffs, QA as a gate, centralized infrastructure, and why speed always feels capped.

This chapter exists to make that assumption visible.

Not a maturity ladder

This book is not saying that organizations must move from traditional to mature to future-ready. You do not have to perfect Agile before moving forward.

You can move directly from traditional to future-ready, or from mature to future-ready. The jump is conceptual, not sequential.

This chapter is not a roadmap. It is a mirror.

The Traditional Engineering Organization

In the traditional organization, engineering is structured around functions and layers. Frontend teams and backend teams are separated. QA exists as a distinct function. Infrastructure and security are centralized.

Automation is partial. Releases are infrequent. Security is enforced by policy rather than internalized by teams.

Product development is often program-driven, especially in B2B contexts. Engineering managers, where they exist, frequently act as delivery coordinators.

This model persists because it is stable. It allows businesses to keep moving.

The Reasonably Mature — but Imperfect — Agile Organization

Organizations mature when engineering speed becomes a visible problem. Agile ceremonies are introduced. Teams become smaller. Product owners and engineering managers appear.

Teams look cross-functional on paper, but dependencies remain. Infrastructure, security, and shared platforms are still centralized.

Agile rituals exist, but clarity varies. Definitions of done and ready are inconsistent. Story points often become proxies for time. Velocity becomes a target.

Automation improves but never fully catches up. Architectures remain mixed. Releases are still gated. Manual work never disappears.

The shared pain

Despite their differences, both organizations suffer from the same complaint:

Engineering feels slow.

Business feels blocked. Engineering feels stretched. Everyone feels constrained.

Why this chapter exists

These organizations are not broken. They are optimized for a world where humans write most of the code, coordination is expensive, validation happens late, and learning is slow.

That world is changing.

This book is not about perfecting either model. It is about questioning the assumption they both share and exploring what a future-ready engineering organization could look like in an AI-shaped world.

This is the only place where we dwell on existing organizational forms. From here on, the question is no longer where you are — but what kind of organization you want to build next.
